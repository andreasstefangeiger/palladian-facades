# Palladian Facades

An open-source Digital Humanities and architectural research project for exploring the geometric grammar, proportional systems, compositional rules, and generative principles underlying Palladian architecture.

## Purpose

This project investigates how Palladian facades can be studied through geometry, proportion, structure, and rule-based composition. It is intended as a public research and educational tool for people interested in architectural form, historical design systems, and the relationship between geometry and architectural meaning.

Many architects, architectural historians, students, and interested readers admire Andrea Palladio's buildings, yet relatively few tools make it easy to examine the deeper structural logic that organizes them. This repository aims to help make that logic visible.

The project focuses on research, education, visualization, and exploration. It is not intended to replace close historical interpretation, archival study, or architectural expertise. Instead, it offers a computational and visual companion to those practices.

## Research Context

This repository is part of a broader research practice guided by a simple principle: every scientific or philosophical research project should produce at least one public-facing contribution.

Such contributions may take different forms, including lectures, publications, public philosophy, open-source repositories, educational tools, or other accessible research outputs. In this case, the repository serves as the public and open-source contribution associated with Palladio research.

The project sits at the intersection of several fields:

- architectural proportion
- facade grammar
- geometric structure
- generative design
- philosophy of form
- natural philosophy
- architectural education
- Digital Humanities

## Why Palladio?

Andrea Palladio's architecture is especially well suited to this kind of study because it combines historical importance with a strong concern for proportion, order, symmetry, and compositional clarity. His buildings and writings have shaped architectural thought across centuries, and they remain central to discussions of classical architecture, rule-based design, and the relationship between beauty and intelligible structure.

Palladian architecture also offers a productive case for computational and visual exploration. Facades, plans, proportional relationships, axes, openings, columns, pediments, and spatial hierarchies can be examined not only as stylistic features, but also as parts of a larger grammar of form.

The aim is not to reduce Palladio's architecture to formulas. Rather, the project asks how geometric and generative tools can help researchers, students, and the public see architectural order more clearly.

## Long-Term Vision

The long-term goal is to develop this repository into a public research environment for studying Palladian form. Over time, it may include visual analysis tools, proportion diagrams, facade comparison features, example galleries, export functions, and educational materials.

The project is deliberately open-ended. It should support both technical experimentation and historically informed interpretation, while remaining accessible to non-specialists. Ideally, it will become useful for teaching, exploratory research, public-facing architectural history, and broader reflection on geometry, form, and order in architecture.

## Current Status

The repository currently contains an early generative SVG-based facade experiment. This existing code provides a starting point for future development, but the project's emphasis is now broader than procedural generation alone.

Immediate priorities include documentation, code cleanup, clearer installation instructions, and a gallery of examples that can communicate the project's research and educational direction.

## Contributing

Contributions are welcome, especially from people interested in architecture, architectural history, Digital Humanities, generative design, visualization, education, or the philosophy of form.

Useful contributions may include:

- documentation improvements
- historical or architectural references
- example facades and visual materials
- code cleanup and modernization
- visualization tools
- educational examples
- research notes and issue discussions

Because the project is research-oriented, contributions should be clear, well documented, and careful about historical or interpretive claims. When possible, please explain the purpose of a contribution and how it supports research, teaching, or public understanding.
